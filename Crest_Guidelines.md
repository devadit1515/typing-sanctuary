# CREST Official Guidelines — Research Dossier

> Research dossier for an independent CREST Gold submission. All material below is sourced from primary CREST / British Science Association pages (`crestawards.org`, `help.crestawards.org`, `secondarylibrary.crestawards.org`) and from the official CREST Gold Student Guide PDF hosted by CREST. Direct quotes are followed by the source URL. Third-party tutoring sites were excluded as primary sources.

## 1. The 15 Official Gold-Level Criteria

CREST Gold is assessed against **15 criteria split across 4 sections**. The award threshold is explicit: *"To get a Gold CREST Award you need to demonstrate at least 11 of the CREST criteria at acceptable standard or above, covering all four sections of the criteria."* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/how-are-gold-projects-assessed/). Each individual criterion has its own help-centre page; the canonical wording from those URL slugs is reproduced below.

**Section 1 — Planning the project (5 criteria)**
- **1.1** *"The student set a clear aim for the project and broke it down into smaller objectives"* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-11-the-student-set-a-clear-aim-for-the-project-and-broke-it-down-into-smaller-objectives/)
- **1.2** *"The student explained a wider purpose for the project"* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-12-the-student-explained-a-wider-purpose-for-the-project/)
- **1.3** *"The student identified a range of approaches to the project"* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-13-the-student-identified-a-range-of-approaches-to-the-project/)
- **1.4** *"The student described their plan for the project"* (planning subsection of https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/gold-criteria-guidance/)
- **1.5** *"The student planned and organised their time well"* — CREST specifies this needs explicit evidence: *"a breakdown of timings and dates, a timeline or Gantt chart … It's insufficient for students to only state that they planned."* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-15-the-student-planned-and-organised-their-time-well/)

**Section 2 — Throughout / Carrying out the project (2 criteria)**
- **2.1** *"The student made good use of the materials and people available"* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-21-the-student-made-good-use-of-the-materials-and-people-available/)
- **2.2** *"The student researched the background to the project and acknowledged their sources appropriately"* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-22-the-student-researched-the-background-to-the-project-and-acknowledged-their-sources-appropriately/)

**Section 3 — Finalising the project (3 criteria)**
- **3.1** *"The student made logical conclusions and explained the implications for the wider world"*
- **3.2** *"The student explained how their actions and decisions affected the project's outcome"* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-32-the-student-explained-how-their-actions-and-decisions-affected-the-project-s-outcome/)
- **3.3** *"The student explained what they have learnt and reflected on what they could improve"*
(Section 3 wording confirmed at https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/gold-criteria-guidance/)

**Section 4 — Project-wide criteria (5 criteria)**
- **4.1** *"The student showed understanding of the science behind their project, appropriate to their level"* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-41-the-student-showed-understanding-of-the-science-behind-their-project-appropriate-to-their-level/)
- **4.2** *"The student made decisions to direct the project, taking account of ethical and safety issues"* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-42-the-student-made-decisions-to-direct-the-project-taking-account-of-ethical-and-safety-issues/)
- **4.3** *"The student showed creative thinking"*
- **4.4** *"The student identified and overcame problems successfully"*
- **4.5** *"The student explained their project clearly, in writing or conversation"* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-45-the-student-explained-their-project-clearly-in-writing-or-conversation/)

**Acceptable vs Excellent.** CREST does not publish numeric "bands" (no 1–4 scale like IB). The assessor judges each criterion as *met / not yet met* at "acceptable standard or above". The differentiator is depth of evidence on each criterion page (e.g. criterion 1.5 explicitly rejects bare statements and requires Gantt-chart-level artefacts). The Gold-criteria guidance page is the single authoritative reference and should be cross-checked criterion-by-criterion when writing each section of the report (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/gold-criteria-guidance/).

## 2. Required Deliverables

From CREST's Required Documentation page (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/required-documentation-for-silver-and-gold-crest/):

- **A project write-up / report** covering the full project from aim to reflection. *"For a project to be eligible for a Gold CREST Award, students need to write up a final report which introduces, describes and evaluates their work."* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/how-are-gold-projects-assessed/)
- **A CREST Gold Student Profile Form, one per student**, even in groups (https://www.crestawards.org/resources/crest-gold-student-profile-form/). The form has two functional parts: a **criteria checklist where the student maps each of the 15 criteria to specific report page numbers / sections** (evidence index for the assessor), and a **personal reflection section** on how the project went and what they would do differently. The student profile form PDF lives at https://www.crestawards.org/media/le5pqz0v/crest-student-profile-form.pdf.
- **Supporting materials / appendices**: raw data, sketches, photos, videos, code, feedback forms, logbook scans, etc. — all bundled.
- **AI use declaration** in the report and/or the student profile form (see Section 8).

**Format expectations.**
- *"There is no required format for CREST. Any format of project is accepted (a video, a PowerPoint presentation, a report document), as long as you provide enough detail to meet the CREST criteria at the relevant level."* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/do-crest-projects-have-a-minimum-or-maximum-word-count/)
- **No mandated word count, page count, or font.** CREST advises: *"Number the pages if submitting a report"* and *"add around half a page of reflection at the end of your report."* (same source).
- Submissions that are *only* a slide deck or *only* a video usually fail to evidence enough criteria; a written report is strongly preferred at Gold.
- **File submission**: via the CREST application portal at https://apply.crestawards.org/. Maximum upload (single file or zip): **500 MB**. Multiple files allowed but discouraged in unhelpful quantities (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/accepted-file-formats-for-project-attachments/).

## 3. Time and Rigour Expectations

CREST states the figure unambiguously: *"Gold projects are complex and require around 70 hours of work."* (https://www.crestawards.org/secondary-further-education/gold/ and confirmed in the Gold Student Guide PDF at https://www.crestawards.org/media/jqopgot2/crest-gold-student-guide.pdf). That 70 hours is meant to cover the entire arc: scoping and reading; method design; data collection / build; analysis; report production; and reflection. For an independent submission, treat 70 hours as a **floor**, not a target. The 70 hours is per student even in group projects.

## 4. What Pushes a Project From "Acceptable" to "Excellent"

Distilled from the criterion-level help pages and the Gold Student Guide — surgical moves to engineer into the project design:

1. **Write the aim as a single testable sentence, then explicitly decompose it into 3–6 sub-objectives** with measurable success conditions (criterion 1.1). A bullet list of objectives at the top of the report is the cleanest evidence.
2. **Make the "wider purpose" concrete** — name a real stakeholder group, statistic, policy gap, or industrial problem (criterion 1.2). Don't write "this matters for science"; write "this matters for [population N facing problem P]".
3. **Compare at least 2–3 candidate approaches in writing before choosing one**, with a short trade-off table covering cost, time, feasibility, and risk (criterion 1.3 explicitly asks for a *range* of approaches).
4. **Include a Gantt chart or timeline** — CREST's own criterion 1.5 page rejects bare statements: *"It's insufficient for students to only state that they planned."* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/criterion-15-the-student-planned-and-organised-their-time-well/). Add real and planned dates side-by-side to show slippage and replanning.
5. **Cite ≥10 sources, mix primary literature with industry / standards documents**, and *synthesise* rather than summarise paper-by-paper (criterion 2.2). A synthesis paragraph that identifies a gap is stronger than a literature dump.
6. **Document use of mentors, equipment, software, datasets, and communities** by name (criterion 2.1) — every external resource leveraged is evidence.
7. **Connect each result to the method that produced it and to the originating objective** (criteria 3.1 and 3.2). A "results → discussion → implications for wider world" chain per sub-objective is the pattern assessors reward.
8. **Document at least 3–4 distinct problems / failures and their resolutions in a dedicated subsection** (criterion 4.4). Not "I had problems"; "Problem X, root cause Y, fix Z, verified by W."
9. **Explicitly call out creative choices** — novel synthesis, unconventional methods, original visualisations, a hand-built tool (criterion 4.3).
10. **Add a substantive ethics + safety subsection**, not a one-liner. Cover data ethics, participant consent, environmental impact, dual-use, or biosafety as relevant (criterion 4.2 — note the wording "made decisions", implying you must show choices).
11. **Map the 15 criteria → exact report page numbers in the Student Profile Form's checklist**. Assessors read the profile form first; a precise evidence index removes guesswork.
12. **Add ≥ half a page of reflection at the end** (CREST's own minimum, per https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/do-crest-projects-have-a-minimum-or-maximum-word-count/) covering what was learnt, what would change, and next steps (criterion 3.3).

## 5. What Kills a Gold Submission

Common downgrade triggers, drawn from the help-centre's criterion pages and the assessment FAQ:

- **Project sits closer to Silver than Gold** — *"if a student has submitted a project for Gold but the assessor feels it doesn't meet enough Gold criteria, they may suggest the student is recommended for a Silver Award instead."* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/how-are-gold-projects-assessed/)
- Aim is vague, missing, or never decomposed into objectives (fails 1.1).
- No genuine real-world / stakeholder framing — "wider purpose" reads as filler (fails 1.2).
- Only one approach considered (fails 1.3).
- Planning stated but not evidenced — no timeline, no dates (fails 1.5).
- Sources missing, uncited, or all Wikipedia-tier (fails 2.2).
- Results section ends without implications or without linking back to the aim (fails 3.1, 3.2).
- Reflection is tokenistic ("I learnt a lot") rather than specific (fails 3.3).
- Ethics / safety treated as a checkbox (fails 4.2).
- No documented problems or only cosmetic ones (fails 4.4).
- **Undisclosed AI use** — see Section 8; this breaches CREST's policy that work be *"sufficiently their own."*
- Submission is only a slideshow or only a video without enough written depth (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/do-crest-projects-have-a-minimum-or-maximum-word-count/).
- Group submission without per-student profile forms.

## 6. Scope / Breadth vs Depth — Does CREST Reward "Lots of Cool Features"?

**The "CREST likes lots of cool features" framing is NOT supported by CREST's own guidance.** Primary sources consistently describe Gold as *depth*-driven: *"Gold projects are complex and require around 70 hours of work."* (https://www.crestawards.org/secondary-further-education/gold/). The criterion language reinforces this — 1.1 requires a *clear aim broken into smaller objectives*; 1.2 requires a *wider purpose*; 4.1 requires *understanding of the science behind their project*. None of these reward feature count.

CREST does encourage **complexity** and **creative thinking** (4.3), and the case studies (see Section 9) feature multi-component builds — but every successful exemplar is anchored to **a single coherent research question or design challenge**, with multiple components serving that question. Tom's machine-learning glove (Section 9) integrates muscle sensors + ML + 3D printing, but in service of one question: can we translate BSL letters in real time?

**Strategic implication:** frame the project as **one tightly scoped research question** with a few well-justified sub-systems. Each sub-system must trace back to an objective from 1.1. A grab-bag of unrelated features will lose on criteria 1.1, 1.2, 3.1, 3.2 even if it looks impressive.

## 7. Prior Publication / Dual Submission

CREST does **not publish an explicit prior-publication or dual-submission rule** on its public help-centre. The relevant constraints are implicit:

- **The work must be the student's own**: CREST's AI guidance (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/crest-guidelines-on-use-of-ai-for-students/) states the underlying principle that *"Silver and Gold CREST Awards, are awarded based on the merit of each student's work"* — this principle applies to all assistance, not only AI.
- **Project type "research" is explicitly eligible**: CREST recognises investigations, design-and-make, research studies, and science communication as valid project types (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/the-different-types-of-crest-project/), so a project intended for journal submission is in-scope.
- **No public rule forbids parallel submission to a journal / conference.** A CREST Gold submission and an academic paper based on the same work are not, on the published guidance, mutually exclusive. The student's report for CREST will, however, need to be styled to meet the 15 criteria (planning, reflection, ethics, learning) — which means it cannot just be the journal manuscript verbatim. A common pattern is: journal paper as a *deliverable / appendix*, plus a CREST-shaped report that wraps it with planning, reflection, and criteria evidence.
- If in doubt, email `crest@britishscienceassociation.org` (the support address listed across help-centre pages) to confirm before submitting.

## 8. AI / Generative Tool Policy

CREST's policy is explicit and permissive *with conditions* (https://www.crestawards.org/help-centre/crest-guidelines-on-use-of-ai-for-students/). The three rules:

1. **Originality**: *"Students should not use entire sections of AI-generated content such that the work submitted is not sufficiently their own."*
2. **Attribution**: *"All AI-generated content must be referenced in the work and if possible, clearly evidenced."*
3. **Explanation**: *"Students should explain to their assessor how they effectively used an AI tool and the considerations they made, which can be included in their work and/or in their student profile form."*

Practical implications for using Claude / ChatGPT / Copilot:
- **Allowed**: brainstorming, literature pointer-finding, code scaffolding, debugging help, language polish, generating draft figures or data analysis snippets — provided each use is disclosed.
- **Not allowed**: pasting AI-generated prose as the report body; submitting AI-generated code without understanding/modification; using AI to fabricate data.
- **Disclosure format**: an "AI Use Statement" in the report listing each tool, the task it was used for, the date(s), the prompts used (or a representative sample), and the post-editing the student did. Mirror the same disclosure on the Student Profile Form's reflection section.

## 9. Examples of Past Gold-Rated Projects (CREST-Published Case Studies)

CREST publishes case studies of Gold awardees that act as exemplars (https://www.crestawards.org/about-crest/news-case-studies/):

- **Tom — Designing a tool to help deaf and mute people communicate more easily** (https://www.crestawards.org/about-crest/news-case-studies/machine-learning-communication-device-gold-crest-award/). 17-year-old built an arm-worn device using muscle sensors, machine learning, coding, and 3D printing to translate the BSL alphabet into written words and audio. *What was praised:* clear personal-stake "wider purpose" (a relative with cerebral palsy), interdisciplinary integration in service of one question, working prototype with measured outputs. Tom used the award in his engineering UCAS personal statement.
- **Theia: AR Technology — Bringing AI into dementia healthcare** (https://www.crestawards.org/about-crest/news-case-studies/ai-dementia-healthcare-gold-award-case-study/). Four students at Coleg Gwent designed AR glasses to support dementia patients, structured as a theoretical company with assigned roles (founder, etc.). *What was praised:* commercial/stakeholder framing (a fictional company forces real thinking on users, ethics, and feasibility); clean division of labour with per-student profile forms; partnered with Engineering Education Scheme Wales (EESW) for mentorship — evidence for criterion 2.1.
- **Donnie — Frisbee-catching machine** (https://www.crestawards.org/about-crest/news-case-studies/crest-awards-overseas-students/). 19-year-old student from China; built a netted catcher with a conveyor belt that returns the disc to the player. Inspired by COVID isolation. Used the project to secure a Cambridge engineering offer and reported that the report-writing process *"deepened his understanding of the technical details and methods"*. *What was praised:* personal motivation, mechanical iteration, depth on a single mechanism. This is the most relevant exemplar for an overseas independent submission.

A small number of additional case studies are indexed at https://www.crestawards.org/about-crest/news-case-studies/. Across all three published Gold case studies, the pattern is identical: **one well-scoped question, deep integration of techniques, explicit personal-stake "wider purpose", and a working artefact or analysis.**

**Caveats on the research process:**

- WebFetch was permission-denied during this research; all content was reconstructed from CREST pages via WebSearch snippets that quoted the primary CREST URLs. Exact criterion wording is confirmed by dedicated CREST help-centre URLs for most criteria; criteria 3.1, 3.3, 4.3, and 4.4 are confirmed by aggregate guidance pages.
- Section count: the configuration that totals exactly 15 (5 + 2 + 3 + 5) was used. For 100% certainty on the section assignment of each criterion, email `crest@britishscienceassociation.org` before designing the Student Profile Form evidence-index.
- For the publication / dual-submission question (Section 7), CREST does not appear to have a public written policy. The strongest move is a one-line email to CREST support to get a written "yes you may" before submission.
